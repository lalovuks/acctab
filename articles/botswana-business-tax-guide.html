Botswana Business Tax Guide (2026)

Corporate income tax, VAT, PAYE, withholding tax, capital gains, BURS deadlines and penalties — everything a Botswana business must know, in one guide.

Updated July 2026 By the ACCTAB Professional Team 15 min read

Tax is where good businesses lose the most money unnecessarily — through missed deductions on one side and penalties on the other. Botswana's system, administered by the Botswana Unified Revenue Service (BURS), is manageable once you understand the handful of taxes that touch a typical business and the calendar they run on.

This guide covers each tax in plain language, the deadlines that matter, the mistakes that trigger penalties, and how legal tax planning — the kind ACCTAB's tax professionals do every day — keeps more of your profit in your business.

Please Note

Tax rates and thresholds are set by government and can change with the national budget. Figures below reflect the position as generally applied at the time of writing — always confirm current rates with BURS or with ACCTAB before making decisions.

Corporate Income Tax

Resident companies pay corporate income tax on taxable income — accounting profit adjusted for non-deductible items and capital allowances — at the standard rate of 22%. Approved manufacturing operations and certain approved activities qualify for a reduced 15% rate, one of Botswana's most valuable incentives for eligible businesses.

Key points:

  • The return (ITA22) is generally due within four months of your financial year end.
  • Companies generally pay tax through self-assessment estimates during the year, not one payment at filing.
  • Capital allowances on vehicles, plant and equipment are a major legitimate deduction — but only if your asset records support them.
  • Losses can generally be carried forward against future profits for a limited period — worthless if returns are not filed.

Value Added Tax (VAT)

VAT applies at the standard rate of 14% on taxable supplies of goods and services, with certain supplies zero-rated (such as exports) or exempt. Registration is compulsory once taxable turnover exceeds P1,000,000 per annum (or is expected to); voluntary registration is possible below the threshold.

  • Registered businesses charge output VAT on sales and claim input VAT on purchases supported by valid tax invoices — paying (or reclaiming) the difference.
  • Returns are generally due by the 25th of the month following the tax period.
  • The most common failures: registering late, claiming input VAT without valid tax invoices, and treating exempt supplies as zero-rated. Each is expensive.

PAYE (Pay As You Earn)

Employers must deduct income tax from salaries using current BURS tables and remit it — generally by the 15th of the following month — plus file an annual reconciliation. Resident individuals enjoy a tax-free band on the first portion of annual income, with progressive rates rising to the top marginal rate. Employment benefits (housing, vehicles, allowances) are frequently taxable and frequently mishandled. Full details in our Complete Payroll Guide for Employers in Botswana.

Capital Gains

Disposals of certain assets — notably immovable property and shares — trigger tax on the gain, computed under specific inclusion rules that differ by asset type. Timing, structure and available exemptions materially change the outcome, so take advice before signing a sale agreement, not after.

Withholding Taxes

For certain payments, the payer must withhold tax at source and remit it to BURS — including dividends (10%), and specified payments such as rent, interest, commission and fees to contractors and non-residents, at varying rates. Two habits keep you safe: check the withholding position before making any new category of payment, and remit what you withhold on time — withheld money is BURS's money, and holding onto it is treated severely.

Penalties: What Non-Compliance Costs

  • Late filing penalties on outstanding returns, per return, accumulating over time.
  • Interest on unpaid tax, accruing until settled — often exceeding the original tax on old debts.
  • Estimated assessments when returns are missing: BURS estimates your income, and the burden shifts to you to disprove it.
  • Blocked tax clearance certificates — quietly the most expensive penalty, as it locks you out of tenders, licences and many contracts.

Tax Planning: Legal and Worth It

Tax planning is arranging your affairs, within the law, to pay no more than you must. Legitimate levers include:

  • Capital allowances — timing asset purchases to maximise relief.
  • Structure — company vs sole trader, and group structure for multi-venture businesses.
  • Expense completeness — every deductible Pula documented and claimed.
  • Incentives — manufacturing rates and other approved-activity reliefs where eligible.
  • Timing — recognising expenditure and income in the right periods.

The line matters: planning is legal; evasion — hiding income, inflating expenses — is a criminal offence. ACCTAB practises the former rigorously and will tell you plainly when something crosses the line.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the corporate tax rate in Botswana?

22% standard, with 15% for approved manufacturing and certain approved activities. Rates can change with the budget — confirm current figures with BURS or ACCTAB.

What is the VAT rate and who must register?

14% standard rate. Registration is compulsory above P1,000,000 annual taxable turnover, and voluntary below it. Thresholds can be revised, so confirm current figures.

When are tax returns due?

Company returns generally within four months of year end; VAT by the 25th of the following month; PAYE remittances by the 15th of the following month.

How does PAYE work for employers?

Deduct income tax from salaries per BURS tables and remit monthly, with an annual reconciliation. The employer is liable for errors and lateness.

What is withholding tax?

Tax the payer must withhold at source on certain payments — dividends, rent, interest, certain fees and payments to non-residents — and remit to BURS.

Does Botswana tax capital gains?

Yes — gains on immovable property, shares and certain assets are taxable under specific inclusion rules. Take advice before any significant disposal.

What penalties apply for late filing?

Penalties per late return plus interest on unpaid tax until settled — and blocked tax clearance certificates, which lock you out of tenders and licences.

What is a tax clearance certificate?

BURS confirmation that your tax affairs are current — commonly required for tenders, licences and contracts. Only compliant businesses can obtain one.

Can I legally reduce my business tax?

Yes — timing, capital allowances, structure and complete deductions. Planning is legal; evasion is criminal. ACCTAB practises only the former.

Do sole traders pay corporate tax?

No — they are taxed as individuals at progressive rates. Which structure is more efficient depends on profit levels; ACCTAB can run the comparison.

What records does BURS require?

Everything supporting your returns — invoices, receipts, bank statements, payroll and VAT records, asset registers — retained for several years.

I have unfiled returns from previous years. What now?

Regularise voluntarily before BURS acts — it is treated far better. ACCTAB handles backlog filings and BURS engagement for you.

Can ACCTAB deal with BURS on my behalf?

Yes — returns, queries, reviews and payment arrangements, all handled as your professional representative.

How is manufacturing taxed differently?

Approved operations can access the 15% rate and other incentives, subject to approval. If you manufacture, an eligibility review is worth its weight in tax.

When should a startup register with BURS?

Immediately on starting business; employer registration when you hire; VAT when you cross or expect to cross the threshold. Late registration means penalties and messy catch-up.
