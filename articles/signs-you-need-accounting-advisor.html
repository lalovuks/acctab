10 Signs Your Business Needs an Accounting Advisor

Businesses rarely fail from one big mistake — they drift into trouble past ten ignorable warnings. Here are the signals we see most often in Botswana, and what each one means.

Updated July 2026 By the ACCTAB Professional Team 9 min read

Most owners call an advisor when something has already gone wrong — the BURS letter has arrived, the investor has walked, the cash has run out. But every one of those crises broadcast warnings first. If any of the ten signs below feels familiar, the cheapest time to act is now. (New to the topic? Start with What Are Accounting Advisory Services?)

The 10 Signs

  1. Growing faster than your systems
  2. Profitable but cash-starved
  3. No budget, no targets
  4. Investors at the table
  5. Expansion on the horizon
  6. Tax getting complicated
  7. Reports that answer nothing
  8. A loan application coming
  9. Compliance slipping
  10. A restructure brewing

1. You're growing faster than your systems

Orders are up, staff are up, chaos is up. Invoices go out late, nobody is sure which jobs make money, and the owner has become the bottleneck for every decision. Growth that outruns working capital and controls is the classic way profitable businesses fail. An advisor puts structure under the growth: cash forecasts, delegated controls, and a reporting rhythm that scales.

2. Profit on paper, nothing in the bank

The income statement says you made money; the bank balance disagrees. The culprits are almost always debtors paying slowly, stock piling up, or drawings and loan repayments exceeding real free cash flow. An advisor traces exactly where the profit is being absorbed — then fixes the biggest leak first.

3. No budget, no targets

If this year's plan is "do better than last year", every surprise arrives unannounced. A budget converts intentions into monthly targets, and variances into early warnings. It is the cheapest management tool that exists, and most SMEs don't have one.

4. New investors or partners at the table

The moment outside money approaches, your numbers, structure and paperwork go under a microscope. Valuation, shareholder agreements, and clean historical accounts decide how much of your own company you keep. Get advice before negotiations — that is when leverage exists.

5. Expansion on the horizon

A second branch, a bigger premises, a new product line. Each looks obvious in optimism and different in arithmetic: break-even points, months of rent before profitability, working capital soaked up in the transition. An advisor models the move so you expand on purpose, not on hope.

6. Tax is getting complicated

Crossing the VAT threshold, hiring staff, importing goods, running multiple entities — each adds interacting BURS obligations, and DIY mistakes compound quietly into assessments. If tax has begun to feel like guesswork, it is; see our Botswana Business Tax Guide, then get a professional in the loop.

7. Your reports answer nothing

You receive statements but cannot tell which product is profitable, which branch is bleeding, or what next quarter looks like. Reporting that only satisfies compliance is half-finished. An advisor redesigns it around your actual decisions.

8. A loan application is coming

Banks decide with documents: financial statements, management accounts, forecasts. Applications assembled in a panic get declined or priced badly. An advisor prepares the pack, anticipates the credit questions, and often changes the outcome entirely.

9. Compliance is slipping

A missed VAT return here, a late annual return there. Each slip seems small; together they build penalties, block tax clearance certificates, and mark you for closer attention. If deadlines are being survived rather than managed, the system — not the effort — is the problem.

10. A restructure is brewing

Partners drifting apart, succession approaching, one company carrying five unrelated ventures, assets tangled between personal and business names. Structural problems never fix themselves; they wait for the worst possible moment. Advisory untangles them deliberately, while all options are still open.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my business is growing too fast?

Sales rising while cash falls, hiring outpacing systems, quality slipping, and the owner losing visibility. Growth that outruns working capital is how profitable businesses fail.

Why is my business profitable but always short of cash?

Usually slow debtors, excess stock, and drawings or repayments exceeding real free cash flow. An advisor traces where profit is absorbed and fixes the biggest leak first.

Do I need an advisor before approaching investors?

Strongly recommended — credible financials, a defensible valuation and protective structure must exist before negotiations, when your leverage is highest.

What does a budgeting process involve?

Monthly revenue and cost targets, actuals compared against them, and action on variances. ACCTAB builds budgets into its monthly reporting rhythm.

When does tax become complex enough for an advisor?

Crossing the VAT threshold, employing staff, cross-border trade, multiple entities, or any BURS query — each adds interacting obligations where mistakes get expensive.

Can an advisor help with a bank loan application?

Yes — preparing statements, management accounts and forecasts, anticipating credit questions, and often improving the terms offered.

What is the difference between my bookkeeper and an advisor?

The bookkeeper records the past accurately; the advisor uses those records to shape the future — pricing, structure, funding, expansion.

How often should I meet an advisor?

Quarterly reviews plus support around big decisions suit most SMEs; fast-growing businesses benefit from monthly contact.

Is an advisor worth it for a one-person business?

At key moments, absolutely — structure, pricing, first employee, equipment purchases. One good decision pays for years of occasional advice.

What happens in the first ACCTAB meeting?

Can ACCTAB advise on restructuring?

Yes — modelling options, quantifying tax and risk, and managing the accounting and secretarial mechanics of the change.

How much does ongoing advisory cost?

Fixed fees scoped after a free consultation, sized to SME budgets. One prevented mistake or one funded application typically pays for the year.
