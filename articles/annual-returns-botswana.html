Annual Returns in Botswana: Everything Directors Need to Know

The one filing that can quietly kill a company. What annual returns are, when they're due, what CIPA requires — and how to never miss one again.

Updated July 2026 By the ACCTAB Professional Team 10 min read

Of every filing a Botswana company must make, the annual return is the most misunderstood — and missing it has the harshest possible consequence: a company that legally ceases to exist. Directors routinely confuse it with the tax return, assume dormant companies are exempt, or believe "the accountant handles it" without ever confirming. This guide clears all of it up.

What Are Annual Returns?

An annual return is a yearly filing with the Companies and Intellectual Property Authority (CIPA) confirming your company's key details: directors, shareholders, registered address, share capital and beneficial ownership. Its purpose is to keep the public register accurate — so anyone dealing with your company (banks, customers, courts) can rely on what the register says.

It is not a tax return. Tax returns go to BURS and report income; annual returns go to CIPA and confirm identity. A company must do both, on separate timetables — see our Botswana Business Tax Guide for the BURS side.

Who Must File

  • Every registered company — private, public, large or single-shareholder.
  • Dormant companies included. Not trading does not suspend Companies Act obligations; many strike-offs happen precisely to dormant companies whose owners assumed otherwise.
  • Responsibility sits with the directors. You can outsource the task; you cannot outsource the duty.

When Annual Returns Are Due

Annual returns run on a yearly cycle tied to the company's registration, filed through CIPA's online system. Because the exact date is company-specific, the practical rule is simple: know your company's cycle, diarise it, and file ahead of it — or use a provider whose compliance calendar does it for you. Waiting for a reminder from CIPA is not a strategy; the penalty arrives faster than the reminder.

Information and Documents Required

  • Current directors — full details, matching your statutory register
  • Current shareholders and shareholding
  • Beneficial ownership information
  • Registered office address
  • Share capital details
  • CIPA online account access in working order

The filing itself is straightforward when the underlying registers are current. It stalls when reality and the register disagree — a director who resigned years ago but was never removed, shares transferred by handshake but never filed. That cleanup is where professional help earns its fee.

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing CIPA and BURS filings — doing the tax return and assuming the company is "compliant".
  • Assuming dormant means exempt.
  • Assuming someone else filed it — the accountant thought the lawyer had it; the lawyer thought the accountant did.
  • Out-of-date registers blocking the filing at the deadline.
  • Lost CIPA account access discovered the day the return is due.

Penalties and the Road to Strike-Off

The consequences escalate in stages:

  1. Late-filing penalties that accumulate the longer the return is outstanding.
  2. A non-compliant public record — visible to any bank, customer or investor doing due diligence, and a blocker for tenders and financing.
  3. Strike-off. Persistent failure can end with CIPA removing the company from the register. The company ceases to exist as a legal person: bank accounts freeze, contracts fall into doubt, assets are jeopardised, and directors face personal complications for continuing to trade.
  4. Restoration — possible, but slow and expensive: applications, all outstanding filings, accumulated penalties, and weeks or months of business paralysis.

Best Practices for Directors

  • Know your annual return cycle and diarise it — with a margin
  • Keep statutory registers current all year, not just at filing time
  • File every change (directors, shares, address) when it happens
  • Keep CIPA account credentials safe and accessible
  • Get written confirmation each year that the return was filed
  • Bundle the whole function into one professional retainer

Frequently Asked Questions

What is an annual return?

A yearly CIPA filing confirming company details — directors, shareholders, address. Not a tax return, and required even if the company did not trade.

Is an annual return the same as a tax return?

No — annual returns go to CIPA (company details); tax returns go to BURS (income). Companies must do both, on different timetables.

When is my company's return due?

On a yearly cycle tied to your registration, filed via CIPA's online system. Dates are company-specific — diarise yours or use a tracked provider like ACCTAB.

Do dormant companies need to file?

Yes — dormancy does not suspend Companies Act obligations. Many strike-offs happen to dormant companies for exactly this assumption.

What happens if I file late?

Accumulating penalties and a publicly visible non-compliant record — a blocker for banks, tenders and investors. Persistent failure leads toward strike-off.

What is strike-off and why is it serious?

Removal from the register — the company legally ceases to exist. Accounts freeze, contracts fall into doubt, assets are jeopardised, and directors face personal complications.

Can a struck-off company be restored?

Generally yes, but it takes applications, all outstanding filings, penalties and time. Prevention costs a fraction of restoration; ACCTAB assists with both.

What information do I need to file?

Current directors, shareholders, beneficial ownership, registered address and share capital — all matching your statutory registers.

Who is responsible if the return is missed?

The directors, legally — outsourcing transfers the task, not the duty. Use a provider that confirms each filing in writing, as ACCTAB does.

We've missed several years. What now?

Does the annual return require financial statements?
